## CHAPTER 1 : INTRODUCTION

## 1.1 Background Study

Food delivery and online ordering systems have become an essential part of modern city
lifestyles particularly for university students and working individuals. While current existing
platforms such as GrabFood, Foodpanda, and ShopeeFood provide ordering features, those
system lack of features that essential for certain of users . Users often face difficulties finding
meals that match their dietary needs, personal preferences, or nearby locations efficiently.
Specifically,  locating  nearby  food  vendor  that  meet  specific  food  requirements  can  be
timeconsuming and confusing. This creates a gap for a system that not only offers online food
ordering  but  also  integrates  precise  location  based  search,  filtering  and  personalized
recommendations.
The  proposed  system,  CraveFood,  aims  to  combine  food-ordering  functionalities  with
mapbased food vendor search, behavioral analysis, and diet-based recommendations to create
a more user-centered experience, with the increasing use of smartphones and web applications,
there is a strong demand for a more advanced food-ordering platform that can understand user
behavior, analyze preferences, and provide accurate suggestions.
This project focuses on developing a web-based platform that helps users discover nearby
restaurants and stalls by filter results based on their needs (budget, cuisine, diet, distance), and
receive personalized recommendations based on their past search patterns. By integrating these
features,  the  system  enhances  accessibility,  convenience,  and  decision-making  for  food
selection, enabling users to quickly find nearby food options that match their preferences ,
thereby reducing the time consumption and confusion when choosing restaurant or stalls based
on user filter or recommendation.





## 1.2 Problem Statement

Difficulty in Discovering Suitable Food in Unfamiliar Campus Areas Students, especially new
intakes or those in unfamiliar faculty buildings, often struggle to identify which cafeterias or stalls
are nearby. Without a centralized directory, they lack knowledge of which vendors are open, what
menus are currently available, or which stalls fit their budget. This information gap forces users
to physically roam the campus searching for food, leading to wasted time between classes and
unnecessary congestion at popular spots while other stalls remain empty.

Inefficiencies  and  Errors  in  Manual  Ordering  Channels  (WhatsApp)  Currently,  many  food
transactions on campus rely on informal, manual communication channels like WhatsApp or
direct verbal ordering. This unstructured process is highly prone to human error. Student orders
often get buried in vendor chat logs, overlooked during peak hours, or accidentally duplicated.
This lack of a centralized tracking system leads to frustration for students who fail to receive their
meals and financial loss for vendors who miss out on potential sales or waste inventory on
redundant orders.

1.3 Aim and Objectives

## 1.3.1 Aim
To develop a web-based food discovery system for the university campus that improves the
accessibility of food information and categorizes meals based on specific health and dietary
requirements to enhance user satisfaction.


## 1.3.2 Objectives
I. To design a system architecture that categorizes campus food vendors and menu
items based on specific dietary requirements (Halal, Vegetarian, Low Lactose)
and price ranges.

II. To develop a dual-mode ordering system that allows users to choose between
immediate "Dine-In" orders or advance "Book" reservations for future pickup.

III. To evaluate the functionality and user acceptance (UAT) of the system among the
university community to ensure it meets dietary search needs.



1.4 Scope of Study
I. The system is focused on digitizing menu information and order management strictly for
food vendors located inside the UMPSA campus.

II. The study focuses on the development of the web-based platform using HTML, CSS,
JavaScript, PHP, and MySQL database.

III. The system can be accessed via standard web browsers on smartphones, tablets, or laptops
without requiring application installation.

IV. The system features a "Choose Option" module that distinguishes between real-time orders
("Dine In") and scheduled orders ("Book"). The "Book" option allows users to secure food
quantity for a specific future date and time.



1.5 Significance of the Project
The  development  of  the  CraveFood  system  provides  key  significance  to  both  users  and
restaurants.

using modern web technology.

look through, filter and select food.












## CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE REVIEW
## 2.1 Overview
This chapter looks at earlier research, systems, and technologies connected to ordering food
online, searching for restaurants on a map, and recommendation systems. This literature review
aims to explore what has already been created, find the flaws in current systems and explain
why this project is important. This review is divided into different topics online food ordering
platforms, the difficulties students encounter when looking for food , map and location systems,
and features that give personalized recommendations.

## 2.2 Food Ordering Systems
Online  food  ordering  services  like  GrabFood,  Foodpanda,  and  ShopeeFood  are  popular
platforms where people can look at restaurant menus, order food, and get it delivered to their
homes. These systems make it easy for people to check restaurant menus and prices without
having to go to the restaurant in person. Many studies point out the shortcomings of these
platforms. Research has found that many users pick food based on how easy it is to get.
However, these platforms often do not provide all the necessary details, like complete menus,
dietary options, or current hours of operation. These systems mainly concentrate on delivery
services instead of assisting users in locating nearby stalls or small vendors that aren't officially
listed on major apps. This shows that even though food ordering systems are popular, they still
don’t have enough clear and current information.
## 2.2.1 Food Ordering System Advantage
Current online food ordering systems provide students with access to choose from many
different restaurants and types of food. Students can look at menus and see prices from home,
making it easy to order food online and have it delivered. Some systems also offer locationbased
search tools that help students find restaurants close to them.




## 2.2.2 Food Ordering System Disadvantage

Although these benefits exist students frequently encounter obstacles when using online food
ordering platforms. The large number of options can complicate the decision of where to eat.
Details about restaurants are occasionally untrustworthy. Hours of operation might be incorrect
or obsolete causing unnecessary trips. Numerous platforms offer menu information online
complicating the process of choosing appropriate dishes. Additionally, students struggle to
identify which restaurants fit, within their budget. New or foreign students who are unfamiliar
with the area depend heavily on online information, so incorrect data can cause frustration and
waste valuable time.

## 2.3 Existing System
GrabFood
GrabFood is a food ordering service embedded within the Grab app enabling customers to order
food  for  delivery  or  collection  from  numerous  restaurants.  The  platform  emphasizes
userfriendliness boasting an structured layout that showcases nearby eateries and trending
meals. Its homepage includes tabs for categories, like local specialties, fast food, sweets and
deals facilitating effortless browsing for users. GrabFood also offers real-time tracking of
deliveries,  letting  users  monitor  the  status  of  their  order  from  preparation  to  arrival.
Additionally, users can manage payment and rewards through the Grab app, earning points for
each transaction that can be redeemed for discounts or free items.




## Foodpanda
Foodpanda is a food delivery platform as a website and mobile app linking users to an extensive
selection of restaurants ranging from neighborhood spots to global franchises. Its interface is
intuitive categorizing menus by cuisine style, cost and review scores to make browsing easier.
Customers can explore restaurant information look at images of meals and access nutritional
facts. Foodpanda offers options, like discounts, coupons and reviews to help users make
informed decisions. The platform is particularly convenient for students and busy individuals,
as it allows ordering with just a few taps. However, some restaurants may have incomplete
menu information, and personalized recommendations are limited.


## KFC
The KFC Web Order is a way to order food from the internet. You can use it on your computer or
phone. You do not have to get an app on your phone. This makes it easy for people to order food
from KFC using their smartphone, tablet or laptop. The KFC Web Order has two options: you can
pick up your food at KFC or they can bring it to your house. This means you can choose to get
your food without waiting in line, at KFC or you can have the KFC Web Order bring it to you.
This platform is really easy to use. It has a feature called "Guest Checkout" that lets users place
orders without having to make an account. The platform finds the users location, on its own to tell
them about the store. It also shows them the menu and the latest deals. Users can easily choose
how they want to get their food like Delivery or Self-Collect. They can pick what food they want
and pay for it online using credit cards or e-wallets. The platform makes it easy for users to order
food from the store. This online-first method makes sure that anyone with internet access can use
the service, no matter how much storage their device has.

## CHAPTER 3: METHODOLOGY
## 3.1 SDLC
This chapter explains the method used to develop the CraveFood system. It includes the
development model, system architecture, tools used, and the main modules of the system. The
purpose of this chapter is to show how the system is planned, designed, developed, and tested
in a structured way.  Waterfall Model
For this project, the Waterfall Model is used. This model is simple and suitable for student
projects because each phase is completed step-by-step.


The phases includes :
## 3.1.1 REQUIREMENT PLANNING
In this phase, the developer identifies all the system requirements to ensure the project meets
the users' needs. This involves understanding the specific problems faced by students, such as
difficulty finding healthy or affordable food on campus. The project is designed to help the
client develop a website that solves these issues by including essential features like nearby
restaurant search, updated menus, filtering options, and personalized recommendation features.



## 3.1.2 SYSTEM DESIGN
The client stated that the website should be simple and user-friendly to assist students in
making quick decisions. This phase focuses on designing the structure of the system to avoid
confusion. It has been advised that the system should have different flows for different users,
so the system is designed with specific diagrams for the database (ERD) and user flow (DFD).
There will be developed interfaces for different types of users, specifically for the general User
(Student) and the Vendor and Admin .


## 3.1.3 DEVELOPMENT
The system development will start in this phase, where the actual coding work is done. The
frontend and backend are developed based on the designs created in the previous phase.
Every task should be completed using specific tools: Visual Studio Code is used for scripting,
MySQL Database is used to store the user and menu data, and Figma is used as a reference for
the interface design. Features like map integration, the login system, and restaurant filtering are
built during this stage.
## 3.1.4 TESTING, DEPLOYMENT AND REVIEW
In this step, the final functions will go through the testing process to be checked for errors.
This includes functionality testing and usability testing to ensuring the website is stable and
reliable. After the coding has been reviewed and the data verified, the system is prepared for
deployment. The website will be hosted locally on a server to be presented as a final product to
the examiners

## 4.4  SYSTEM AND USER INTERFACE IMPLEMENTATION

## 4.4.1 CUSTOMER (USER) INTERFACE

Homepage & Recommendations (Homepage.php):
The primary landing page features a personalized recommendation engine. It queries the user's order
history to identify their most-ordered DietaryTag and Category (falling back to profile preferences if
history is empty) to display 8 personalized food items.



Cart Management (Cart.php & CartActions.php):
The  cart  utilizes  server-side  sessions  ($_SESSION['cart']).  A  strict  single-vendor  validation  is
implemented via JSON API, ensuring customers can only add items from one vendor at a time.


Checkout & Order Types (OrderOption.php):
Users configure their orders before place order. The interface adapts based on the selected Order Type.



Real-Time Chat (Chat.php):
A communication interface that allows users to message vendors regarding active orders. It utilizes
JavaScript polling (fetch every 3 seconds) to load new messages incrementally. The chat input is
automatically disabled once the order status changes to Completed or Cancelled.




## 4.4.2  VENDOR INTERFACE


Vendor Dashboard (VendorDashboard.php):

Displays the vendor's active menu in a data table with full CRUD (Create, Read, Update, Delete)
capabilities. It also renders dismissible banner alerts if the vendor receives an update notification from
the admin.


Order Management (VendorOrders.php):
A live-updating module (auto-refreshing every 30 seconds) that lists incoming orders. Vendors use
dropdown menus to progress the order lifecycle through specific states: Pending to Preparing to
Cooking to Ready to Completed. If an order is cancelled, a mandatory reason must be provided.


## 4.4.3 ADMIN INTERFACE




Admin Dashboard (AdminDashboard.php):
Display system statistics (total users, vendors, and orders).

Vendor Monitoring (AdminNotifyVendor.php):
The core administrative function tracks the LastUpdate timestamp of vendor profiles. Vendors who
have not updated their profiles in over 30 days are flagged. The admin can trigger individual or bulk
AJAX requests to dispatch system alerts to these outdated vendors.






## 4.5 USER ACCEPTANCE TEST (UAT)

4.5.1 User Interface and System Usability


The evaluation of the system’s overall User Interface (UI) yielded highly positive results. The
data shows that a combined 70% of respondents agreed that the system is clean and easy to
navigate  (40%  Agree,  30%  Strongly  Agree).  This  indicates  that  the  unified,  tabbed
authentication  design  (AuthHandler.php)  and  the  structured  layout  of  the  food  catalog
successfully provide an intuitive browsing experience. Only a small minority (10%) disagreed,
suggesting minor room for layout optimizations in future iterations.

## 4.5.2 Personalized Food Recommendations


The personalized recommendation engine located on Homepage.php was evaluated for its
effectiveness in matching user dietary tags (e.g., Halal, High Protein). Interestingly, the results
indicated  a  neutral  reception,  with 80%  of  respondents  selecting “Neutral” and  20%
disagreeing. This suggests that while the backend algorithm successfully fetches data based on
user history, the frontend presentation of these recommendations may not be prominent or clear
enough to significantly impact the user's purchasing decision. This finding provides a valuable
basis for future enhancements in the system's UI hierarchy.

4.5.3 Single-Vendor Cart Restriction


To prevent fragmented order tickets, the CraveFood system enforces a strict single-vendor
policy via CartActions.php. When evaluating the clarity of this restriction, 60% of users
responded positively (50% Agree, 10% Strongly Agree), while 30% remained neutral. This
majority consensus validates that the system's error-handling prompt is understandable to end-
users, successfully justifying the database schema design that binds a single cart session to a
specific vendor ID.

4.5.4 Checkout and Order Modalities

The checkout module (OrderOption.php) is one of the most critical components of the system.
The UAT results demonstrated strong user satisfaction, with 80% of respondents agreeing
that the process is straightforward (60% Agree, 20% Strongly Agree). This confirms that the
dynamic form inputs—which successfully adapt to capture seat counts for Dine-In or date/time
parameters for Reservations—are highly effective and user-friendly.



4.5.5 Real-Time Active Order Chat



The real-time communication module (Chat.php) was designed to eliminate miscommunication
during  food  preparation.  The  survey  results  were  exceptionally  positive,  with 80%  of
respondents validating its effectiveness (50% Agree, 30% Strongly Agree) and the remaining
20% remaining neutral. There were zero negative responses. This high acceptance rate proves
that the 3-second JavaScript polling mechanism successfully bridges the communication gap
between students and vendors during active orders.

## 4.5.6 Overall System Preference

The  final  metric  evaluated  the  overall  acceptance  of  the  digitized  platform  compared  to
traditional methods (e.g., physical queuing or manual messaging). The data shows that 50% of
the respondents prefer the CraveFood system, while 40% are neutral, and only 10% disagree.
This positive lean indicates that the system successfully addresses many pain points of campus
food management, though the high neutral percentage suggests that integrating automated
payment gateways in the future could be the key to fully replacing manual ordering methods.





## 4.6 DEVELOPMENT CHALLENGES , STRENGTH AND WEAKNESSES

Throughout the implementation and testing phases of the CraveFood Service System, several
technical challenges were encountered. Analyzing the final prototype also reveals specific strengths
in  the  system's  architecture,  as  well  as  areas  with  potential  weaknesses  that  require  future
enhancement.

## 4.6.1 STRENGTHS OF THE SYSTEM


The primary strength of the developed prototype lies in its direct alignment with the project’s
initial objectives to improve student health and campus dining efficiency.

## • Dynamic Dietary Filtering:
Unlike  standard  food  delivery  applications  that  only  categorize  food  by  type  (e.g.,
"Beverages" or "Snacks"), the CraveFood system successfully implements a DietaryTag
architecture within the menu_food database. This allows the system to execute complex SQL
queries in the AdvanceSearch.php module, giving students the precise ability to filter meals
based on exact nutritional needs, such as "Protein" or "Vegetarian."

## • Effective Congestion Mitigation:
The system's back-end logic successfully separates 'Dine-In' orders from 'Book'
(reservation) orders. By enforcing a mandatory PickupTime for reservations via JavaScript
and PHP validation in the ProcessOrder.php module, the system actively prevents cafeteria
crowding by allowing vendors to schedule food preparation efficiently.

- Secure and Scalable Architecture:
The use of PHP secure sessions and password_hash() ensures that student data is protected,
while the relational MySQL database is designed to scale easily if more vendors or students
are added to the platform.








## 4.6.2 WEAKNESSES OF THE SYSTEM

While the system achieves its core 60% development milestone, there are recognized limitations in
the current build:

## • Vendor Data Dependency:

The accuracy of the health filters relies entirely on vendors correctly inputting the DietaryTag
when adding new food items. If a vendor mislabels a food item or leaves the tag blank, the
Advance Search feature will not retrieve it, which could limit the user's options.

## • Basic Recommendation Algorithm:
Currently,  the  Personalized  Recommendation  engine  in  Homepage.php  uses  a
straightforward rule-based algorithm (matching the user's saved DietaryPreference directly
to food tags). It does not yet utilize advanced machine learning or track past order history to
generate complex, dynamic suggestions.


## 4.6.3 CHALLENGES ENCOUNTERED DURING DEVELOPMENT

Transforming the system design into a working web application presented several significant
challenges:
- Google Maps API Integration:
A major challenge was integrating the Google Maps JavaScript API with the PHP backend
to  calculate  vendor  distances.  It  required  complex  logic  to  capture  the  user's  HTML5
Geolocation coordinates, pass them securely to the map script, and dynamically render a
visual radius (radiusKm) overlay while simultaneously filtering the database results.
- Handling Dynamic Order Types:                                                                          Managing the
database schema to handle different types of transactions within a single orders table was
difficult. Designing the interface to dynamically show or hide the PickupTime input based on
the dropdown selection required careful synchronization between client-side JavaScript (for
the UI) and server-side PHP (to block invalid submissions).
- Database Relational Integrity:                                                                                      Setting
up the foreign keys across the user, menu_food, and orders tables posed a challenge during the
initial database construction. Ensuring that deleting a food item or user did not cause cascading
crash errors in the order history required careful configuration in phpMyAdmin.

## CHAPTER 5 : CONCLUSION
## 5.1 SUMMARY
The CraveFood Service System is an online food ordering platform created to help university
students and staff with their dining problems. Even though apps like GrabFood and Foodpanda
are widely used, they usually aren't the best fit for a campus setting. Students often find it hard
to locate meals that meet their nutritional needs, like high-protein foods for building muscles
or fiber-rich dishes for a balanced diet. They frequently waste time looking for suitable places
to eat because they have limited information. CraveFood aims to help people find healthy food
options easily. The system uses a simple "Order & Pick-up" model, which removes delivery
fees and helps decrease crowding in the cafeteria. The platform stands out because of two smart
main features CraveFood offers a Advance Search feature with Health Filters that lets users
find vendors based on their specific dietary requirements, which is different from regular
apps.Users can easily find food that supports their lifestyle by looking for options based on
protein, fiber, price, and distance. Personalized Recommendation , A mixed system looks at
what you like to eat and what you’ve ordered before to recommend meals that fit your tastes
instead of giving you random ads. The system is created using the Waterfall Model and depends
on a strong database. It has automated alerts that help keep vendor menus current, addressing
the  frequent  problem  of  outdated  information.  CraveFood  focuses  on  nutrition  and  gives
personalized recommendations, making it a better and affordable choice for students who want
to eat healthy while at school.

## 5.3 LIMITATIONS
Despite  successfully  meeting  all  project  objectives,  the  study  and  the  developed  system
encountered a few general limitations:
- Time  Constraints: The  allocated  timeframe  for  this  Final  Year  Project  restricted  the
development  phase.  Due  to  this  time  limitation,  the  system  focuses  strictly  on  core
functionalities (ordering, chat, and recommendations) and could not integrate highly complex
secondary features like a fully automated financial payment gateway.
- Platform  Compatibility: The  system  is  currently  developed  as  a  responsive  web-based
application. While accessible via mobile browsers, it lacks the deep device integration (such as
native push notifications) that a dedicated native smartphone application (iOS or Android)
would provide.

- Environment Scope: The system is currently optimized and tested within a localized server
environment (XAMPP). It has not yet been subjected to real-world, large-scale stress testing
with thousands of concurrent users, which would be necessary for a full university-wide
deployment.


## 5.4 RECOMMENDATIONS FOR FUTURE WORK
Based on the limitations identified and feedback gathered during User Acceptance Testing,
several enhancements are recommended for future iterations of the CraveFood system:

- Integrated In-App Navigation:
To significantly improve the overall user experience, future versions should feature a built-in
campus map navigation system. Currently, users may need to rely on external map applications
to find specific vendor stalls. Upgrading the system to provide seamless, in-app routing and
directions—without forcing the user to leave the CraveFood platform—would greatly enhance
convenience, especially for new students.

## 2. Native Mobile Application:
Developing  a  dedicated  mobile  application  would  allow  the  system  to  utilize  native
smartphone features, providing instant push notifications for order status updates and vendor
chat messages, thereby reducing the reliance on web browser polling.

## 3. Payment Gateway Integration:
Integrating a secure, third-party online payment gateway (such as ToyyibPay, FPX, or Stripe)
would fully automate the financial transaction process, eliminating the need for manual cash
handling during food pickup.









## 5.5 FINAL CONCLUSION
In conclusion, the CraveFood Service System successfully addresses the inefficiencies and
miscommunications associated with manual campus food ordering. The project met all stated
objectives by delivering a functional, multi-vendor web application equipped with dynamic
dietary filtering, real-time communication, and administrative monitoring. The findings from
the  functional  and  user  acceptance  testing  demonstrate  that  applying  full-stack  web
technologies  can  significantly  enhance  operational  efficiency  and user  satisfaction.  The
outcomes of this study highlight the practical importance of software engineering in solving
real-world logistical problems within educational institutions, providing a strong foundation
for future smart-campus developments.
